1. Project Summary

Clinical problem: Patients in a bedridden state have reduced mobility and severe or no speech capabilities, making them unable to efficiently communicate their basic needs[cite: 2122].

Value proposition: The system uses electrooculography (EOG) signals to translate eye movements into automated commands sent in real time via IoT, providing a non-invasive, accessible, and low-cost communication alternative[cite: 2123].

3. Clinical Data Management and Flow

Data pipeline

Continuous streaming: The EOG signal changes in real time according to eye movement relative to the electrodes[cite: 2149].

On-device preprocessing:

  • Differential amplification (high CMRR) [cite: 2151]
  • Low-pass filtering [cite: 2152]
  • Comparators for direction detection [cite: 2153]
  1. It begins with high-gain (1000) differential amplification using the AD620 with a gain resistor close to 47 Ohms. [cite: 2154]
  2. The next step is filtering, using an active low-pass filter with a cutoff frequency between 50–52 Hz to eliminate high-frequency noise. [cite: 2155]
  3. Finally, analog comparator configurations are used to establish voltage thresholds to discretize direction. [cite: 2156]
Transmission format: JSON, sent to the cloud (Grafana) for dashboarding. No permanent storage on the device. [cite: 2157, 2158]

4. Regulations and Compliance

  • IEC 60601 (electrical safety): Critical for the hardware stage. Since electrodes are placed on the head, isolation must be ensured to prevent electric shock or leakage microcurrents[cite: 2176].
  • ISO 13485 (quality management): Applies to best practices in the design and manufacturing of the biomedical instrumentation prototype[cite: 2177].
  • Data protection (HIPAA-like): Although patient records are not captured, the Wi-Fi channel must be secure to protect privacy within the home[cite: 2178].

Out-of-scope regulations:

  • FDA approval [cite: 2181]
  • COFEPRIS: Health registration [cite: 2182]

Device classification: Class II: A non-invasive diagnostic/monitoring device whose malfunction could stress or affect the patient's level of care[cite: 2184].

6. No Use of Artificial Intelligence

Why is rule-based or threshold logic sufficient?

EOG is directly linear with gaze angle for deviations up to approximately 30°[cite: 2210]. Using operational amplifiers configured as voltage comparators establishes efficient static thresholds for direction (e.g., positive = right, negative = left) without complex algorithmic processing[cite: 2211].

Is there clinical evidence supporting these thresholds?

Yes, clinical guidelines from the American Academy of Sleep Medicine (AASM) support lateral electrode placement and document that eye movements generate opposite polarities that are easily identifiable and measurable using fixed thresholds[cite: 2213].

Risks avoided by not using AI:

Model bias, lack of data, and black-box effects are avoided. It eliminates algorithmic bias and dependency on prior data collection for training across different patients[cite: 2215, 2216].

When would AI be appropriate in the future?

If there is a need to decode rapid patterns, classify complex noise (such as intentional blinks), or use smartphone cameras (computer vision) to eliminate facial electrodes[cite: 2218].

Advantages:

Greater transparency, lower computational cost, easier auditing[cite: 2220]. The circuit behavior is fully predictable and explainable, with reduced latency and simpler validation under medical hardware regulations[cite: 2221].

7. Users, Clinical Context, and Ethical Considerations

User profile: Primary: Patient. Secondary: Caregiver, family member, or healthcare staff[cite: 2223, 2225, 2226].

Usage environment: Home[cite: 2227].

Informed consent:

Before implementing the device, a form will be provided to the patient or legal guardian explaining in clear, non-technical language the non-invasive nature of EOG biopotential collection, the purpose of transmitting commands to the cloud, the technical limitations of the prototype, and the commitment to secure data handling[cite: 2229].

Clinical risk analysis

False positive False negative Consequence
A blink or muscle spasm activates the threshold and sends an unintended command[cite: 2231]. Caregiver fatigue and alarm overload[cite: 2232].
The patient performs the eye movement, but a dry electrode or baseline drift hides the signal[cite: 2233]. Patient distress due to unmet physiological needs[cite: 2234].

Equity considerations:

EOG measures the retinal dipole, so it is independent of skin tone, ethnicity, or facial visual characteristics[cite: 2236].

8. Scope, Limitations, and Future Work

In scope:

Delivery of an analog prototype that captures horizontal and vertical eye biopotentials. Includes wireless transmission to an IoT server and real-time visualization in Grafana with basic alert triggering[cite: 2239, 2240].

Out of scope:

Medical certifications, commercial PCB design and manufacturing, and mobile app development[cite: 2242]. This is due to time constraints, limited financial resources, and clinical regulations requiring extensive testing and validation[cite: 2243].

Hardware or data model limitations

Noise, user movement, electrode dependency[cite: 2245].

Future Roadmap

  • Improve signal filtering[cite: 2247].
  • Develop a mobile app capable of detecting eye movements using the phone camera without relying on electrodes[cite: 2248].
