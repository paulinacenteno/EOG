EOG Assist System

Electrooculography IoT Communication System

1. Project Summary

Clinical Problem

Individuals who remain bedridden often experience significant limitations in movement and may lose the ability to speak partially or entirely. As a result, they struggle to communicate their basic needs effectively.

Value Proposition

This project proposes a system based on electrooculography (EOG), which captures eye movement signals and converts them into predefined commands. These commands are transmitted in real time through IoT technology, offering a communication method that is non-invasive, affordable, and easy to implement.

2. Device / Sensor / IoT Equipment Description

Sensors and Components:
  • AD620 instrumentation amplifier
  • TL084 operational amplifiers
  • Arduino microcontroller
  • Ag/AgCl surface electrodes
Physiological Variable:

Electrical potential generated by eye movement (EOG).

Units: Volts (V, mV)

Measurement Specs:

Operating range: 10 µV to 5 mV
Minimum resolution: 10 µV

Communication:

Wireless connection via WiFi. Protocols such as HTTP or MQTT for cloud communication.

Sampling Rate:

250 Hz, sufficient for capturing low-frequency EOG signals (below 50 Hz) without aliasing.

Power Supply & Consumption:

Analog circuitry: ±12 V
Communication module: 3.3-5 V
Consumption: Low, estimated under 500 mW.

3. Clinical Data Management and Flow

Data Acquisition: The EOG signal is continuously monitored, reflecting changes caused by eye movement relative to electrode placement.

On-device preprocessing:

  • Differential amplification with high common-mode rejection
  • Low-pass filtering to remove unwanted high-frequency noise
  • Voltage comparison to determine gaze direction

Processing Steps:

  1. The signal is first amplified using the AD620 with a gain of approximately 1000.
  2. A low-pass active filter (cutoff around 50 Hz) is applied to reduce noise.
  3. Comparators define voltage thresholds to classify movement direction.
Data Transmission & Storage

Data is formatted in JSON and sent to a cloud platform (Grafana). No local storage is implemented on the device.

Retention & Access

Retention time is short (~30 seconds). No personal patient data is stored; only system-generated commands are logged.

Audit & Integrity

Cloud system records events with timestamps, detected actions, and user identifiers. Original signals remain unchanged.

Error Handling: Possible issues include signal noise saturation or incorrect electrode placement.

4. Regulations and Compliance

  • IEC 60601: Ensures electrical safety, especially important due to electrode contact with the patient.
  • ISO 13485: Guides quality management during design and development.
  • Data protection practices: Secure WiFi communication is required to maintain privacy.
Not included:

FDA or COFEPRIS certification.

Device Classification:

Class II medical device, as failures could impact patient care.

5. IoT System Architecture

System Layers:

  • Perception Layer: Electrodes, AD620, TL084, Arduino
  • Connectivity Layer: WiFi module integrated with the microcontroller
  • Processing Layer: Edge (basic signal interpretation) & Cloud (visualization using Grafana)
  • Application Layer: Dashboards and alerts for caregivers

Non-functional Requirements:

  • Latency below 1 second
  • System availability around 95%
  • Designed for a single user
  • Battery-powered for continuous operation

6. No Use of Artificial Intelligence

Why is rule-based or threshold logic sufficient?

EOG signals have a linear relationship with eye movement (up to ~30°), allowing the use of fixed voltage thresholds to determine direction.

Is there clinical evidence supporting these thresholds?

Standards from the American Academy of Sleep Medicine confirm that eye movements produce measurable polarity changes detectable with electrodes.

Risks avoided by not using AI:

Model bias, Lack of data and Black-box effects are avoided.

Advantages:

Greater transparency, lower computational cost, easier auditing. The circuit behavior is fully predictable and explainable, with reduced latency and simpler validation under medical hardware regulations.

When would AI be appropriate in the future?

Could be useful for detecting complex patterns, filtering noise, or replacing electrodes with camera-based systems.

7. Users, Context, and Ethics

Users & Environment:

Primary: patient
Secondary: caregivers or healthcare personnel
Environment: Designed for home use

Consent & Equity:

Consent: Patients or guardians must be informed about how the system works, its limitations, and data handling practices.
Equity: EOG signals are not affected by skin color or physical appearance, making the system broadly applicable.

Clinical Risk Analysis

False positive False negative
Unintended commands due to involuntary movements Failure to detect intended movement
Effect: caregiver fatigue Effect: unmet patient needs

8. Scope and Limitations

In Scope:
  • Functional analog prototype
  • Detection of eye movements
  • Wireless transmission and real-time visualization
Out of Scope:
  • Medical certification
  • Commercial manufacturing
  • Mobile application development

Limitations:

  • Signal noise
  • Dependency on electrode placement
  • Sensitivity to user movement

Future Improvements:

  • Better signal filtering
  • Development of a mobile app using camera-based detection
